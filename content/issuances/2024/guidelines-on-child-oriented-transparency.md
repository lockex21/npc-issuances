## Issuance Text

**Advisory No. 2024-03**

WHEREAS, Section 7 (g) of [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012|Republic Act No. 10173]] or the [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012|Data Privacy Act of 2012]] empowers the National Privacy Commission (NPC) to publish, on a regular basis, guides to laws relating to data protection; Section 9 of the [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012|DPA]]’s [[laws/implementing-rules-and-regulations-of-the-data-privacy-act-of-2012|IRR]] authorizes the NPC to develop, review, or amend rules for the effective implementation of the [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012|DPA]]; and Section 20 of the [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012|DPA]] requires Personal Information Controllers (PICs) to implement reasonable organizational, physical, and technical security measures appropriate to the risks associated with the processing of personal data.

WHEREAS, a “child” is a person below eighteen (18) years of age or those eighteen (18) or over but unable to fully care for themselves or protect themselves from abuse, neglect, cruelty, exploitation, or discrimination because of a physical or mental condition; children, as data subjects and rights holders, are entitled to meaningful access to information and extra protections when the NPC or PICs process their personal data;

WHEREAS, recognizing children as rights holders with evolving capacities means child-focused decisions must emphasize the best interests of the child; and WHEREAS, Sections 16 and 11 of the [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012|DPA]] and the Principle of Transparency require PICs to inform data subjects (including children) about the nature, purpose, scope, risks, safeguards, and rights connected to data processing, presenting that information in plain language while retaining necessary technical terms;

WHEREFORE, in consideration of these premises, the NPC issues this Advisory on the Guidelines on Child-Oriented Transparency.

**SECTION 1.** ***Scope.*** This Advisory applies to PICs and Personal Information Processors (PIPs) that process children’s personal data in digital or physical environments, including products or services specifically intended for children or likely to be accessed by them.

**SECTION 2.** ***Risk-Based Assessment.*** Children’s data must be processed consistent with the general privacy principles. PICs must keep children aware of the nature, purpose, and extent of processing in accordance with the Principle of Transparency, and adopt a risk-based, child-oriented approach aligned with Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) best practices.

  A. **Child Privacy Impact Assessments (CPIAs).** PICs shall integrate CPIAs into their PIAs before launching products or services intended or likely to be accessed by children, and thereafter as needed. The PIA is an ongoing requirement, reviewed and updated whenever there are changes in products, services, processes, or regulations. Factors to consider include:

   1. Purpose of processing  
    2. Types of data to be processed (collected, used, disclosed)  
    3. Sources of data  
    4. Technologies or systems employed  
    5. Data storage and disposal  
    6. Data sharing  
    7. Security measures implemented  
    8. Impact of the processing activity on children  
    9. Age range of likely users  
   10. Process owner within the PIC or PIP  
   11. Risk identification  
   12. Involvement of parents or guardians when heightened risks exist

   The level of parental or guardian involvement should match the risk, and PICs must document how they secure and verify that involvement.

  B. **Risk Mitigation Measures.** PICs must address identified risks while keeping the best interests of the child and the Principle of Transparency at the forefront. Possible measures include:

   1. **Age Assurance Mechanisms.** PICs may deploy age assurance tools to determine user age range, recognizing that self-declaration alone may be insufficient for high-risk use cases. Children should understand why the age-check exists, and the processing must have a lawful basis and comply with general privacy principles.[^9]
    2. **Privacy Controls.** PICs must adopt enhanced privacy controls proportionate to the risk:
        a. **High Privacy Settings by Default:** Children’s accounts must default to the most protective settings (e.g., disable geolocation, set profiles to private, and minimize data sharing).[^10]
        b. **Easy Access to Settings:** Privacy controls must be clearly explained, easy to find, and enable children to manage their privacy preferences while maintaining baseline protection.

**SECTION 3.** ***Privacy Notice.*** PICs must ensure children understand the nature, purpose, and extent of processing. Privacy Notices should be readable, tailored to children’s comprehension and evolving capacities, and accessible in multiple formats (videos, infographics, animations, audio) for different age ranges. PICs must describe:

  A. Specific processing activities, purpose, lawful basis, risks, importance of privacy settings, rights as data subjects, and any updates to the Privacy Notice.  
  B. Age-appropriate communication that is concrete, definitive, and linguistically accessible to intended users, even when the product also targets adults; child-friendly notices should accompany standard ones when children are involved.[^14]  
  C. Just-in-time notice: inform children with minimal, context-relevant data before processing occurs.  
  D. Layered notices that direct children to supplemental content separated from adult-targeted material, always in child-comprehensible language.[^16]  
  E. A prohibition on deceptive design patterns, coercion, or influence from trusted characters that would push children to disclose more data than necessary.[^17]

**SECTION 4.** ***Data Breach Notification.*** When mandatory breach notification applies, PICs must notify children and their parents or guardians, using language children can understand, even when the event occurs outside mandatory frameworks.[^18][^19]

**SECTION 5.** ***Accountability.*** PICs bear responsibility for children’s personal data, including when they subcontract processing. The best interests of the child must guide decisions on age assurance, content evaluation, privacy notices, and enhanced security and privacy controls.[^20]

**SECTION 6.** ***Interpretation.*** Any doubt about this Advisory must be resolved in a manner mindful of children’s rights and evolving capacities, giving full effect to their best interests.
