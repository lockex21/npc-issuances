## Issuance Text

**Circular on Guidelines for Legitimate Interest**
**Questions Raised During the Public Consultation on 07 December 2023**

**SECTION 1.** ***General Questions.***  
1. **What is legitimate interest?** Section 3 (A) of the Circular defines legitimate interest as an actual and lawful benefit, advantage, or gain that a PIC or third party seeks through processing. The purpose must remain lawful, not contrary to morals, public order, or public policy, and the activity should be transparent to data subjects.

2. **When does legitimate interest apply?** It is a valid lawful basis under Section 12(f) of the [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012|DPA]] provided sensitive personal information is not involved. Legitimate interest may co-exist with other bases, but whichever basis applies must be communicated to the data subject under Section 16 of the [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012|DPA]] and Section 34 (a)(2)(c) of the IRR.

3. **Whose interests matter?** The legitimate interest of the PIC or the third party that receives the data is considered per Section 12(f). Section 3(C) clarifies that a third party is anyone other than the PIC, PIP, or the data subject, and the general privacy principles continue to apply even when legitimate interest is claimed.

4. **Is a privacy notice still required?** Yes. Transparency requires stating the lawful basis—whether consent, legitimate interest, or another ground—regardless of the basis claimed.

5. **Does legitimate interest replace consent?** Not necessarily. Legitimate interest can be used instead of consent, but the PIC must still provide transparency notices, including through consent forms if those are in scope.

6. **Is there a transition period?** Section 8(B) allows a 90-day transition for documenting legitimate interest assessments. The period only covers the recordkeeping requirement because the three requisites already exist in Section 12(f); PICs should either have assessments on record or quickly prepare them.

**SECTION 2.** ***Protecting the Data Subject.***  
7. **Can legitimate interest be used to shield fraudsters or private data?** Legitimate interest cannot be a cover for withholding critical details in fraud investigations. The public’s interest in pursuing legal remedies may override that of a wrongdoer, provided privacy laws such as bank secrecy continue to be respected.

8. **How is proportionality ensured?** The PIC must discuss with requesting parties what information is truly necessary and document any refusal. Proportionality balances the request against the requestor’s purpose, not just the PIC’s perspective.

**SECTION 3.** ***Third-Party Interests.***  
9. **May a PIC rely on a third party’s assessment?** Yes. The PIC may document the third party’s legitimate interest so long as all three requisites are present and properly recorded.

10. **Must both parties have assessments?** No. Either the disclosing PIC or the receiving third party can demonstrate the legitimate interest; duplication is unnecessary.

11. **Can legitimate interest be a ground for refusal?** Refusal should rest on proper legal grounds; relying on a lack of legitimate interest without analysis may be problematic. If no lawful basis exists, the PIC must explain why.

12. **Is there a prescribed verification procedure?** No. The Circular allows any existing structure or exercise, including email threads and annexes, as long as the three requisites are documented.

13. **Does the third party need to notify the PIC before further processing?** No. Once the third party processes the data, it becomes a PIC itself and is subject to the same obligations.

**SECTION 4.** ***Requisites for Legitimate Interest.***  
14. **Must all elements in Sections 5–7 be satisfied?** Yes. The three requisites—legitimate interest, necessity, and non-override of rights—must all be met for the specific processing.

15. **What does “necessary” mean?** It refers to proportionality: the means must be adequate and not excessive. The purpose (first requisite) and the means (second) together determine the effect on a data subject.

16. **Are implemented safeguards enough?** Evidence of safeguards helps satisfy the second requisites, but PICs must still show effectiveness through good-faith assessment.

17. **How do we judge impact?** The effect depends on the combination of purpose and means. Factors include the impact on the data subject, mitigation steps, alternative methods, and the subject’s reasonable expectations.

18. **Is Section 7 a restatement of Section 4?** No. Section 7 emphasizes assessing the combined effect of purpose and means, for example, evaluating whether debt collection is carried out discreetly or publicly.

19. **What if a less intrusive method exists?** The PIC must choose the least intrusive means it can reasonably afford while still meeting its purpose. Resource constraints and business realities are part of the evaluation.

20. **What is a “reasonable expectation”?** It is what a reasonable person would accept under the specific circumstances, especially for further processing, and is distinct from the Ople standard.

21. **Is the right to object absolute?** No. It exists alongside legitimate interest, so data subjects must be informed and given an opportunity to object under [[laws/implementing-rules-and-regulations-of-the-data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-34-rights-of-the-data-subject|Section 34(b) of the IRR]].

**SECTION 5.** ***Legitimate Interest Assessment (LIA).***  
22. A legitimate interest assessment documents how the PIC meets the requisites, providing evidence for the purpose, necessity, and balance of interests.

23. No separate guidelines exist; Sections 5–7 contain the needed clarity.

24. There is no prescribed form. The assessment may be a standalone document, part of a privacy impact assessment, or spread across annexed materials.

25. Email or message trails can serve as acceptable proof if they clearly demonstrate the three requisites.

26. The NPC may request the assessment during investigations or compliance checks.

27. Assessments should be reviewed regularly; they are not one-time submissions. Any organizational or process change warrants a refreshed assessment.

28. Incorporating the LIA into the privacy impact assessment is encouraged since it evaluates the same processing context.

29. Documentation should be retained for as long as the processing activity is ongoing and available upon request.

**SECTION 6.** ***Further Processing.***  
30. Further processing of consented data may rely on legitimate interest, provided a new assessment covers the additional purpose.

31. If consent still applies to that new purpose, the PIC may continue relying on it rather than switching bases.

**SECTION 7.** ***Operational Notes.***  
32. Legitimate interest may not be the only lawful basis. The PIC should always choose the most appropriate lawful basis and explain it to data subjects.

33. The transitory period in Section 8(B) only affects recordkeeping.

34. The Circular clarifies existing law; it does not change obligations related to transparency, notice, or the right to object.
