The case falls under mandatory data subject notification, as provided

by the provisions of NPC Circular No. 16 -03.

Rule V, Section 11 of NPC Circular No. 16 -03 provides:

Sec. 11. When notification is required .

Notification shall be required upon knowledge of or when there

is reasonable belief by the personal information controller or

personal information processor that a personal data breach

requiring notification has occurred, under the following

conditions:

A. The personal data involves sensitive personal information

or any other information that may be used to enable identity

fraud. For this purpose, “other information” shall include,

but not be limited to: data about the financial or economic

situation of the data subject; usernames, passwords and other

login data; biometric data; copies of identification documents,

licenses or unique identifiers like Philhealth, SSS, GSIS, TIN

number; or other similar information, which may be made the

basis of decisions concerning the data subject, including the

grant of rights or benefits.

B. There is reason to believe that the information may have

been acquired by an unauthorized person ; and

C. The personal information controller or the Commission

believes that the unauthorized acquisition is likely to give

rise to a real risk of serious harm to any affected data

subject .21  (Emphasis supplied)

It must be noted that the personal data involved in this case includes:

• First and las t names of clients (no middle names).

• Booking details, i.e., ticket numbers, destinations, flight

schedules, and the like.

• Email addresses (not applicable to all data subjects).

• Telephone numbers (not applicable to all data subjects). 22

Further, NPC Circular No. 16 -03 Section 11 provides that other

information is not limited to the categories listed therein but applies

also to any information that , under the circumstances, would and can

be used to enable identity fraud.

In this case, the above -mentioned data when taken together, are

considered other information that may be used to enable identity

fraud. 23  Considering that these pieces of information when combined

with other information , “would directly and certainly identify an

individual” are considered as other information under the

circumstances that may result to enable identity fraud. 24

In this case , the names and email addresses of the affected data subjects

can be used to commit identity theft or any other phishing activities.

Here, the Commission emphasizes that the risk of this information at

the use and disposal of an unauthorized person is beyond the control

of TravelPeople . The names and email addresses may be used to carry

out other motives to obtain more information about the affected data

subjects due to the unauthorized acquisition of this information.

As to the second requisite, the third -party forensic investigation

engaged by TravelPeople stated that “there was no malicious

executable files dropped on the victim machine” and “there were no

outbound connections to any known malicious hosts that were seen in

the packet capture as signs of command and control of the

miscreant.” 25  In line with this, TravelPeople stated that “the

information could not have been acquired by an unauthorized person

as a consequence of the ransomware”. 26  The third -party forensic

investigation also claimed that there is no data exfiltration as a result

of the ransomware attack. However, it must be noted there is nothing

on the submission to substantiate its claim that , indeed no data

forensic investigation is a mere allegation without any proof to

support its claim.

It should be noted that a loss of control over personal data held

in custody should be enough for a personal information

controller to have “reason to believe that the information may

have been acquired by an unauthorized person.” An indication

of exfiltra tion of data is not a requirement in Section 11(b).

Absolute certainty of acquisition by an unauthorized person is

not required by either the Circular or the Data Privacy Act (DPA),

considering that the condition only provides for a determination

based on the existing circumstances that can give a “reason to

believe.” 27

In this case, TravelPeople has lost control over the information of its

clients. It must be noted that TravelPeople admitted that “booking

records of approximately [one hundred thousand] 100,000 or more

individuals became temporarily inaccessible” as a result of the

ransomware attack in its system. 28  Hence, it can be inferred that there

is a loss of control over personal data when TravelPeople has lost

access to its system.

Moreover, in a previous Resolution issued by the Commission, it held

that:

Ransomware is defined as “a type of malicious software that

infects a computer and restricts users’ access to it until a ransom

is paid to unlock it… Typically, these alerts state that the user’s

systems have been locked or that the user’s files have been

encrypted. Users are told that unless a ransom is paid, access will

not be restored.”16 While ransomwares primarily caus e

availability breaches, it is different from other availability

breaches because a malefactor intentionally causes them. This is

unlike other types of availability breaches that are caused by

accidents or system glitches. In these cases, the total exercise of

control over the data is removed from the personal information

controller and is taken by the malefactor. Without this control,

the personal information controller will be unable to exercise it

obligations in processing the p ersonal data according to the

provisions of the DPA. Recent ransomware attacks have also

shown a capability to release the encrypted data over the internet

upon non -payment of the ransom, potentially leading to a

confidentiality breach contemplated in Section 11(2). For the

Thus, the second requisite is present in this case considering that due

to the ransomware attack, the loss of control over the information of

the data subjects would be a reason to believe that the information may

have been acquired by an unauthorized person.

Finally , Travel People claimed that no harm was inflicted on the data

subjects as a consequence of the incident . However, it must be noted

that mere temporary inconvenience to Travel People ’s staff in

manually inputting the booking records of approximately one

hundred thousand ( 10 0,000) or more individuals is not the “harm”

contemplated under Section 11(c) of NPC Circular 16 -03. The serious

harm contemplated under Section 11 of the NPC Circular No. 16 -03

means negative consequences it may pose to the affected data subjects

such as identity fraud or theft that may result from the ransomware

attack. 30

As to the third requisite, given that the incident involves information

that , under the circumstances, may be used to enable identity fraud,

and that such information may have been acquired by an

unauthorized person wherein PIC has lost its control over the

information , as discussed above, there is reason for the Commission to

believe that the unauthorized acquisition may give rise to real risk of

serious harm to the affected data subjects. Hence , the third requisite is

present.

In view thereof, considering that all the requisites for mandatory

breach notification are present in this case, the Commission deems it

proper to direct Travel People to notify the affected data subjects.
