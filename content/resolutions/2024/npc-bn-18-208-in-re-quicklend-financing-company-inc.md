### Facts
On 05 November 2018, Quicklend reported to the National Privacy

Commission (NPC), through its Complaints and Investigation

Division (CID), a possible data breach after receiving an information

from one of its clients on 30 October 2018 , prompting Quicklend to

review their emails .1

Quicklend thereafter discovered an email sent to its clients on 10

October 2018 , advising them to maintain a minimum amount based on

their monthly dues :

“Greetings from Quicklend, Inc.! This is to kindly remind you to

maintain your loan payment amount in your payroll account

which is due for Auto -Debit Deduction on October 15, 2018.

Please be informed that there will be a daily Php50.00 penalty fee

while your Auto -Debit Deduction is unsuccessful due to

insufficient funds. Thank you for your due diligence in

cooperating with us and helping us avoid possible collection

problems and additional finance charges.” 2

### Issue
I.  Whether the incident falls under mandatory breach

notification; and

II.  Whether Quicklend has sufficiently complied with the Order

dated 26 October 2020 , including the implementation of

reasonable and appropriate security measures.

### Discussion
The Commission finds that the incident does not fall under mandatory

breach notification and Quicklend has sufficiently complied with the

Order dated 26 October 2020 , including the implementation of

reasonable and appropriate security measures . As such, the

Commission resolves to close the matter.

I. The matter does not fall under

mandatory breach notification.

Section 11, NPC Circular 16 -03 provides when notification is require d:

SECTION 11. When notification is required.

Notification shall be required upon knowledge of or when there

is reasonable belief by the personal information controller or

personal information processor that a personal data breach

requiring notification has occurred, under the following

conditions:

A. The personal data involves sensitive personal

information or any other information that may be used to

enable identity fraud. For this purpose, “other information”

shall include, but not be limited to: data about the financial


In re: Quicklend Financing Co mpany, Inc .

or economic situation of the data subject; usernames,

passwords and other login data; biometric data; copies of

identification documents, licenses or unique identifiers like

Philhealth, SSS, GSIS, TIN number; or other similar

information, which may be made the basis of decisions

concerning the data subject, including the grant of rights or

benefits.

B. There is reason to believe that the information may have

been acquired by an unauthorized person; and

C. The personal information controller or the Commission

believes that the unauthorized acquisition is likely to give

rise to a real risk of serious harm to any affected data

subject. 19

In this case, the first element of mandatory breach notification is

present because names and email addresses may be used to enable

identity fraud . Unauthorized acquisition of these information could

expose the affected data subjects to harassment, discrimination, or

other risks of real and serious harm such as phishing attacks which

involve emails containing links to a fake website intended to steal the

recipient’s personal data.

The second element is also apparent because t here is reason to believe

that the information may have been acquired by an unauthorized

person as the names and email addresses of the other clients were

exposed to parties not privy to the ir correspondence with Quicklend,

after all the other email addresses were inadvertently placed in the

“TO ” field.

However, the third element is absent because the unauthorized

acquisition is not likely to give rise to a real risk of serious harm to any

affected data subject. The Commission has previously held:

To determine whether there is a likelihood that the unauthorized

acquisition of information will give rise to a real risk of serious

harm, there shall be a relation between the incident and any

possible resulting harm to affected data subjects. The risk shall

be apparent and not a product of mere speculation.

Consequently, “serious harm” means that the consequences and

effects to any affected data subject i s significant based on the

surrounding circumstances of the breach. 20

> V, § 11 (15 December 2016) (NPC Circular 16 -03).

To recall, the breach occurred by reason of the “clinical error ” by one

of Quicklend’s employees which resulted in the exposure of the names

and email addresses of one hundred thirty -six (136) clients to the other

email recipients .21

On the same day, Quicklend was immediately alerted by one of its

clients who received the email regarding a possible breach. 22  This only

shows that said client was adamant in protecting her personal

information and consequently that of the other clients as well .

After receiving the report from one of its clients, Quicklend review ed

its emails and thereafter learned of the breach incident .23  Said

discovery caused Quicklend to notif y the Commission on 05

November 2018 and subsequently the affected data subjects on 07

November 2018 .24

These actions , coupled by the fact that the nature and amount of

information involved was limited to the names and email addresses of

Quicklend’s client s and the security measures it has em ployed ,

mitigated the extent of potential misuse and exposure of information

involved in the breac h.

Thus, notwithstanding that the emails contained information that may

enable identity fraud , such unauthorized acquisition did not give rise

to a real risk of serious harm to any of them.

sufficient, reasonable, and appropriate

measures to address the incident.

Section 20 (a) and (b) of the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (DPA) provides:

Sec. 20. Security of Personal Information.

(a) The personal information controller must implement

reasonable and appropriate organizational, physical and

Breach ).

• On the same day, management issued a memo reminding

staff to be vigilant handling batches of email addresses.

Furthermore, an additional protocol was added calling for a

peer review of emails before they are sent out (attachment:

In view of the foregoing, the Commission finds that the matter does

not fall under mandatory breach notification and the measures

undertaken by Quicklend have sufficiently addressed the breach to

prevent a recurrence of the same.
