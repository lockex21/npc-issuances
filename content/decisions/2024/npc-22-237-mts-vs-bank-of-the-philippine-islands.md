DE JESUS, *D.P.C.*;

Before the Commission is MTS' complaint against Respondent BPI – ZBC (BPI) for alleged violation of [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-25-unauthorized-processing-of-personal-information-and-sensitive-personal-information|Section 25 (Unauthorized Processing)]] and [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-26-accessing-personal-information-and-sensitive-personal-information-due-to-negligence|Section 26 (Access Due to Negligence) of the Data Privacy Act]] or Republic Act No. 10173 (DPA).[^1]

### Facts

In her Complaints-Assisted Form (CAF) dated 26 October 2022, Complainant narrated that around 4:00 o'clock in the afternoon of 05 August 2022, she received a phone call from a person alleging to be a customer service representative of BPI Card Services.[^2]

According to the Complainant, the caller claimed that she had earned a reward of Nine Thousand Pesos (P9,000.00) "to be put to [her] account as earned reward on [her] BPI Visa/Mastercard."[^3] Complainant further narrated that the caller mentioned her card number which led her to believe the caller was a legitimate employee."[^4]

The Complainant later revealed that the caller managed to access her account, change the password, and withdraw PHP 50,000.00 from her savings account on six (6) separate occasions.[^5]

According to the Complainant:

> BPI was not able to help me return any amount of money knowing that this is a legitimate fraud. They said "VFishing". Not sure what that is. I am a US/Citizen Dual [and] have been here for 2 years. Not familiar who the real people [and] employee of BPI. Not sure why my credit card/account was hacked by her. My Information was hacked and BPI stated to me that is not their problem at all since OTP was provided. The fraudster is really forceful [and] believable. Why [is] BPI not secure my information at all.[^6]

Complainant believes that BPI should be held responsible in returning her money back.[^7] Complainant posited that "BPI should shoulder part of P300,000.00 regardless since "fraud" is committed although there [is] part negligence [and] ignorance on both parts."[^8]

To prove her allegations, Complainant attached the following to her CAF[^9]:

1. Cardholder Dispute Form from BPI;[^10]
2. Complainant's handwritten statement dated 08 August 2022;[^11]
3. A printout of the Complainant's transactions issued by BPI MH, CB, showing that on 05 August 2022, a total amount of Three Hundred Thousand Pesos (P300,000.00) was transferred from her account to ShopeePay Philippines;[^12]
4. An email from BPI showing that transactions with ShopeePay were made;[^13]
5. An email from Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) dated 15 August 2022 acknowledging receipt of a duly accomplished BSP Consumer/Inquiry/Request for Assistance (CIR) Form;[^14]
6. Complainant's duly accomplished BSP CIR Form;[^15]
7. BSP Complaints/Inquiry/Follow-Up/Rejoinder Form;[^16]
8. Email from Shopee Philippines Email Support stating that it can no longer cancel or refund the transactions that were completed in real-time;[^17] and
9. Email from BPI dated 18 August 2021 stating that access to Complainant's banking account requires logging in using a username and password; that the fund transfers were authenticated using an OTP, which was an internationally accepted security standard that also passed BSP's regulatory standards; and that it can no longer reverse the transaction since the transactions were authenticated from Complainant's end.[^18]

Finally, Complainant prayed in her CAF for BPI to reimburse her Three Hundred Thousand Pesos (P300,000.000), *to wit*:

> My Information was hacked and BPI should reimburse my PhP 300,000.00 [that was] lost[.] and [BPI should] settle with me. Fraud is fraud[, it is] committed regardless if I have given OTP. BPI [was] not able to protect my information and was able to get used by Fraudster[^19]

On 01 March 2023, BPI, with the assistance of counsel,[^22] filed its Comment dated 27 February 2023.[^23]

In its Comment dated 27 February 2023, BPI acknowledged that the Complainant was its client.[^24] BPI shared that on 11 August 2022, Complainant filed a case against Respondent before the BSP alleging six (6) unauthorized transactions in her account totaling Three Hundred Thousand Pesos (P300,000.00.)[^25]

According to BPI, on 18 August 2022, it replied to the said complaint via email and informed the Complainant of the results of its investigation on the disputed transactions.[^26] The pertinent portion of this email reads:

Financial transactions also require further authentication by a One-Time PIN (OTP), sent through a text message to your registered mobile number, or Mobile Key. Any transaction that is done by using your username and password and duly validated either through your One Time Pin (OTP) or your Mobile Key is presumed to be done by you.

These are all considered valid as these were properly authenticated from your end.[^27]

BPI, in its Comment, further denied the allegation of the Complainant that it violated [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-25-unauthorized-processing-of-personal-information-and-sensitive-personal-information|Section 25 (Unauthorized Processing) of the DPA]].[^28] BPI argued that "during account opening, the Complainant expressly agreed to be bound by the provisions of the Terms and Conditions of BPI Products, Services, Facilities and Channels."[^29]

BPI added that "aside from the Complainant's consent, the processing of her personal data was necessary for her continued use of the Respondent's services, facilities and channels. Hence, contrary to the Complainant's allegations, the foregoing discussion categorically proves that she expressly authorized the Respondent to process her data."[^30]

As to the allegation of violation of [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-26-accessing-personal-information-and-sensitive-personal-information-due-to-negligence|Section 26 (Access Due to Negligence)]] of the DPA, BPI claims that "the Complainant miserably failed to present even a shred of competent evidence establishing that the Respondent was negligent in processing her data, and the same was the proximate cause of the fraudulent transactions. Verily, the burden of proof necessarily lies with the party making the allegation."[^31]

To establish its diligence, BPI narrated in its Comment that it "implements a multi-factor authentication method to verify transactions made through BPI Online. This requires the concurrence of the following personal data conclusively presumed to be known only to the depositor:[^32]

> a. BPI Online username;
> b. BPI Online password; and
> c. one-time-password ("OTP," for brevity) sent to the depositor's registered mobile number, or Mobile Key."[^33]

BPI added that the "disputed transactions would not have been made without the concurrence of the foregoing personal data. Therefore, the transactions are conclusively presumed to be made by the Complainant herself. She has the burden to present competent evidence to prove otherwise. Bare self-serving allegations do not equate to proof."[^34]

BPI, in its Comment also claimed that assuming arguendo that there were indeed unauthorized transactions, the reasonable presumption is that the Complainant was guilty of gross negligence and should solely bear the loss, to wit:

> The Complainant, as a data subject, has the obligation to safeguard her personal data. Sharing them with an unknown third person is tantamount to gross and inexcusable negligence. Hence, the Complainant must solely bear the consequences. She simply cannot shift the loss to the Respondent through an unfounded and farfetched accusation that her rights as data subject were violated.[^35]

BPI also shared its policy not to inquire into its client's OTP and regularly reminds its clients to safeguard their personal data,[^36] which reads:

> To protect its clients and raise awareness on personal data security, the Respondent frequently reminds its clients that it will never require them to verify or divulge their personal data such as bank account number, log-in credentials, credit card details, and OTP via email link, text message or phone call. These advisories are regularly sent to its clients' registered email addresses and mobile numbers. They are also posted on Respondent's official website, and its various social media accounts.[^37]

Finally, BPI prays for the dismissal of the Complaint as it is "bereft of any sufficient information to establish a cause of action against the Respondent under Sections 25 and 26 of the Data Privacy Act."[^38]

### Issue

Whether BPI violated [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-25-unauthorized-processing-of-personal-information-and-sensitive-personal-information|Section 25 (Unauthorized Processing)]] and [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-26-accessing-personal-information-and-sensitive-personal-information-due-to-negligence|Section 26 (Access Due to Negligence)]] of the DPA.

### Discussion

The Commission resolves to dismiss the instant Complaint for lack of substantial evidence. BPI cannot be held liable for a DPA violation given the allegations and evidence provided by Complainant.

Section 6 of Rule 133 of the 2019 Amendments to the Revised Rules on Evidence provides:

> Section 6. *Substantial Evidence.* In cases filed before administrative or quasi-judicial bodies, a fact may be deemed established if it is supported by substantial evidence, or that amount of relevant evidence which a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to justify a conclusion.[^82]

In *NIC v. Guanzon*,[^83] the Court elaborated on the standard of proof required before administrative or quasi-judicial bodies, defining substantial evidence as:

> [T]he quantum of evidence required to establish a fact in cases before administrative or quasi-judicial bodies. It has been defined as "such amount of relevant evidence [that] a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to justify a conclusion." This quantum of evidence "is satisfied where there is reasonable ground to believe that [a person] is guilty of the act or omission complained of, even if the evidence might not be overwhelming.[^84]

While Section 1 of Rule 131 of the 2019 Amendments to the Revised Rules on Evidence provides for the definition of burden of proof:

> Section 1. *Burden of proof and burden of evidence.* **Burden of proof is the duty of a party to present evidence on the facts in issue necessary to establish his or her claim or defense by the amount of evidence required by law**. Burden of proof never shifts.
>
> Burden of evidence is the duty of a party to present evidence sufficient to establish or rebut a fact in issue to establish a prima facie case. Burden of evidence may shift from one party to the other in the course of the proceedings, depending on the exigencies of the case.[^85]

This principle that the duty to present evidence sufficient to support one's claim remains with the party making the allegation is well-established in jurisprudence. The Supreme Court has consistently held that "whoever alleges a fact has the burden of proving it because a mere allegation is not evidence."[^86]

In *Government Service Insurance System v. Prudential Guarantee*[^87] the Supreme Court affirmed that "it is basic in the rule of evidence that bare allegations, unsubstantiated by evidence, are not equivalent to proof. In short, mere allegations are not evidence."[^88]

Thus, the Complainant in this case bears the burden of proof to substantiate her claims. However, she failed to show, by substantial evidence, that BPI committed a violation of the DPA.

#### *Respondent is not liable under Section 25 of the DPA due to lack of substantial evidence.*

To be held liable under [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-25-unauthorized-processing-of-personal-information-and-sensitive-personal-information|Section 25 of the DPA]], the following requisites must concur:

> 1. The perpetrator processed the information of the data subject;
> 2. The information processed was personal information or sensitive personal information;
> 3. The processing was done without the consent of the data subject, or without being authorized under the DPA or any existing law.[^89]

As to the first requisite, under the DPA, processing is defined as "any operation or any set of operations performed upon personal information including, but not limited to, the collection, recording, organization, **storage**, updating or modification, retrieval, consultation, use, consolidation, blocking, erasure or destruction of data."[^90]

According to Complainant, the alleged fraudster knew her name, address, date of birth, and her card number.[^91] The Complainant claims the alleged fraudster accessed her account, changed the password, and withdrew PHP 50,000.00 from her savings account on six separate occasions, transferring the amount to ShopeePay Philippines.[^92] The Complainant equates the fraudster's knowledge of her personal details and entry of this information into ShopeePay Philippines to "retrieval" and "use" of personal information, which she argues constitutes "processing" as defined above.[^93]

To support her claims of unauthorized processing of her name, date of birth, and her card number against BPI, the Complainant presented several pieces of evidence: a screenshot of the OTP text messages, correspondences with BPI and Shopee PH email support, and a printout of transactions from BPI Maharlika Highway, Cabanatuan Branch.[^94] The printout shows a transfer of PHP 300,000.00 from her account to ShopeePay Philippines on 05 August 2022.[^95]

However, aside from these documents, the Complainant did not demonstrate that BPI was responsible for processing her personal data (i.e., authenticating, retrieving, and using it) in connection with the alleged unauthorized transactions. It is important to emphasize that mere allegations do not constitute proof.[^96] In other words, there was no sufficient proof to substantiate Complainant's claim of unauthorized processing by BPI of said personal data.

The Commission finds that Complainant's submissions fail to establish a connection between the alleged processing and BPI's involvement. Although there was processing of personal data involved, the Complainant failed to prove by substantial evidence that BPI processed her personal information, specifically her name, date of birth, and her BPI Visa/Mastercard card number[^97], and due to or through this processing, the fraudulent caller obtained the same.[^98]

As such, since the Complainant failed to substantiate that BPI processed her personal data in connection with the alleged unauthorized transactions, which is the first requisite for a violation of Section 25 of the DPA. As a result, the Respondent cannot be held liable for unauthorized processing.

#### *Respondent is not liable under Section 26 of the DPA due to lack of substantial evidence.*

As to the alleged violation of [[laws/data-privacy-act-of-2012#section-26-accessing-personal-information-and-sensitive-personal-information-due-to-negligence|Section 26 (Access Due to Negligence)]], Complainant also failed to sufficiently prove that the unauthorized access to her information was brought about by the negligence on the part of BPI.

Anent this claimed negligence by BPI, Complainant asserts BPI's negligence on account that there was "[n]ot enough protection BPI provided to [my] account knowing that we have a scammer/fraudster here in the Philippines and it is getting wild, and that BPI "did not provide enough notice not to entertain such a call."[^99] However, these claims are unsupported by concrete evidence. The Complainant has failed to substantiate her allegations with proof of a violation of Section 26 of the DPA.

In contrast, BPI has demonstrated its measures in place in safeguarding customer information. One of which is thru its multi-factor authentication method for transactions conducted through BPI Online, which requires personal data presumed to be known only to the account holder.[^100] Additionally, BPI has issued communications instructing its customers not to share their OTPs, as evidenced by the attached documents: "*Bank of the Philippine Islands, Outsmart Fraudster Today*"[^101], "*Bank of the Philippine Islands, BPI Encourages its Clients to be More Cybersafe with Digital Security Features*,"[^102] and notably, the screenshots of OTP messages included in the Complainant's Memorandum,[^103] which explicitly advise customers to "NEVER share [their] OTP and password with anyone."[^104]

Moreover, as correctly pointed out by BPI, BSP itself warns the public of vishing attacks through its primer entitled Protect Yourself from Fraud and Scam[^105], to wit:

> In its primer entitled Protect Yourself from Fraud and Scam, the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas ("BSP," for brevity) defined voice phishing or vishing as "voice calls, automated voice recording, or Voice over Internet Protocol (VoIP) from someone pretending to be an employee of a bank or popular company, and asking for account details." The BSP warns the public not to provide their personal information, account details, or passwords to anyone, even if they claim to be representatives of banks or financial institutions. This is because banks or financial institutions will never ask for the said information.
>
> It must be stressed, however, that vishing attacks related to online banking accounts in the country are usually not targeted attacks. Generally, they are random calls looking for clients of a particular financial institution. In this day and age, it is easy for cybercriminals to obtain mobile numbers, email addresses, or other personal information since people regularly share them with personal and business contacts, establishments, social media platforms, and various websites. In fact, government issued identification cards, email addresses, credit card information, online accounts, and other personal information are being traded by cybercriminals in the dark web market.[^106]

Consequently, the lack of substantial evidence to support the Complainant's allegations precludes any conclusion that the access to the Complainant's information, which resulted in the disputed transactions, was due to BPI's negligence. Therefore, BPI cannot be held liable under Section 26 of the DPA.

Given the foregoing, the Commission finds that BPI cannot be held liable for violating Section 25 (Unauthorized Processing) and Section 26 (Accessing Due to Negligence) of the DPA.

Although this case is being dismissed due to insufficient substantial evidence implicating BPI in the alleged DPA violations, this decision should not be construed as a conclusion to the broader imperative of advancing data protection practices. Rather, it serves as a reminder of the ongoing necessity for continual improvement in security and efficiency within the banking sector.

In light of this, financial institutions, who are imbued with public interest and are required to exercise the highest degree of diligence, are strongly encouraged to move beyond the mere issuance of reminders about the risks associated with fraudulent calls and other security threats. While such notifications play a role in raising awareness, they are insufficient on their own to effectively combat the rising prevalence of financial fraud.

To adequately address and mitigate evolving threats, it is crucial that banks should explore other measures to protect their clients. Proactive measures and innovative strategies are essential for safeguarding personal data, thereby fulfilling the enduring obligation to protect both operational integrity and customer information against sophisticated and emerging risks.

**WHEREFORE,** premises considered, the Complaint of MTS against BPI – ZBC is hereby **DISMISSED** for lack of substantial evidence

This is without prejudice to the filing of appropriate civil, criminal or administrative cases against Respondent BPI before any other forum or tribunal, if any.
