Issue

Whe ther Respondent Bank of the Philippine Islands is liable under

the DPA.

Discussion

The Commission dismisses the complaint for lack of substantial

evidence .

Respondent did not commit a

violation of the DPA .

Preli minarily, it is observed that Complainant did not specify a

particular provision of the DPA violated by the Respondent in her

CAF 43  or in the Complaint -Affidavit 44  attached . However, regardless

of the designation of the act allegedly committed by Respondent, the

Commission deems it proper to dismiss the charge against

Respondent considering that Complainant failed to provide su fficient

evidence to prove any participation whatsoever on the part of

Respondent in the alleged disclosure of her personal data .

In Avelina Sagun v. Sunace International Management Services, Inc. ,45  the

Supreme Court ruled that in proceedings before quasi -judicial bodies ,

the quantum of evidence necessary to establish one’s claim is

substantial evidence:

In proceedings before administrative and quasi -judicial

agencies, the quantum of evidence required to establish a fact is

substantial evidence, or that level of relevant evidence which a

reasonable mind might accept as adequate to justify a

conclusion. 46

Moreover , Section 1 of Rule 131 of the 2019 Amendments to the

Revised Rules on Evidence provides for the definition of burden of

proof:

Section 1. Burden of proof and burden of evidence . Burden of proof

is the duty of a party to present evidence on the facts in issue

necessary to establish his or her claim or defense by the

amount of evidence required by law . Burden of proof never

shifts.

Burden of evidence is the duty of a party to present evidence

sufficient to establish or rebut a fact in issue to establish a prima

facie case. Burden of evidence may shift from one party to the

other in the course of the proceedings, depending on the

exigencies of the case. 47  (Emphasis supplied.)

Further, in Gatmaytan v. Dolor ,48  the well -established principle

regarding the burden of the party alleging a fact is concisely restated :

As basic as the previously -discussed principles on appeal as a

statutory privilege, finality of judgments, and service of papers,

is the principle that "a party who alleges a fact has the burden of

proving it." A mere allegation will never suffice: "a mere

allegation is not evidence, and he who alleges has the burden of

proving the allegation with the requisite quantum of evidence."

Logically, a party who fails to discharge his or her burden of

proof will not be entitled to the relief prayed for. 49

Thus, it is Com plainant who has the burden of proving her claim

against Respondent by substantial evidence .

In this case, what appears to have transpired was a phishing incident.

Phishing is defined as the fraudulent process of attempting to acquire

private or confidential information by masquerading as a

trustworthy entity in an electronic communication. 50  The

responsibility for the avoidance of falling victim to phishing falls

both on the personal information controller (PIC) and the data

subject. 51

In Complainant’s narration, however, it appears that her personal

data ha s already been obtained by the threat actor prior to

communicating with Complainant, and was in fact relayed by the

threat actor to her to make her believe that the fraudster was an

authorized representative . Nothing in Complainant’s submissions

prove the fact that it was Respondent who was the cause of the

disclosure of Complainant’s information to the threat actor. T he mere

fact that a threat actor knows Complainant’s personal data is not

proof that the Respondent disclosed her information.

Aside from Complainant’s allegations in her CAF 52  and Complaint -

Affidavit 53  that Respondent disclosed her data to a third person, the

only other evidence submitted by Complainant serves to prove the

fact that transactions involving her banking account occurred, and

that Complainant communicated the matter to Respondent for the

purpose of requesting assistance. 54  Taken together, the evidence

submitted by Complainant do not prove , by substantial evidence,

participation by Respondent in the disclosure of Complainant’s data,

which led to the allegedly fraudulent transactions.

Considering the Complainant’s failure to discharge her burden to

prove by substantial evidence, any form of participation on the part

Notwithstanding the foregoing, the Commission takes this

opportunity to impress upon the banking sector the weight of their

responsibility as keepers of their depositor’s personal data. As PICs

controlling the processing of information pertinent to financial

transactions, banks process personal data which, if mish andled, may

result in their depositor’s economic ruin, among others.

While the holding of information campaigns may be an effective

means to spread awareness among depositors regarding the

prevalence of phishing and other similar scams, the holding of the

same should not be deemed a license allowing banks to evade

liability borne from their failure to satisfactorily fulfill their solemn

duty to safeguard their depositors’ personal data .

The Commission stresses that the holding of these campaigns should

not have the effect of passing the banking sector’s burden onto their

depositors. To adequately address the rise of online financial scams,

banks must take a more direct and active role in helping prevent the

occurrence of these scams. As such, banks are strongly encouraged to

innovate technical and proactive solutions to reduce the likelihood of

depositors being victimized.

WHEREFORE, premises considered, this Commission hereby

resolves that the instant complaint filed by KDC against the Bank of

the Philippine Islands is DISMISSED for lack of substantial

evidence.

This is without prejudice to the filing of appropriate civil, criminal or

administrative cases against the Respondent Bank of the Philippine

Islands before any other forum or tribunal, if any.
