PRIVACY POLICY OFFICE
                             ADVISORY OPINION NO. 2025-0131

09 October 2025




           Re:       VERIFICATION OF INFORMATION SUBMITTED
                     FOR VISA APPLICATION

Dear

We write in response to your inquiry on the processing of sensitive personal information in
the context of international migration requirements considering the provisions of Republic
Act No. 10173, also known as the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (DPA), its Implementing Rules and
Regulations (IRR) and relevant issuances of the National Privacy Commission (NPC).

We understand that Dataflow Verification Services Ltd. (Dataflow) is involved in performing
Primary Source Verifications. Your concern arose from the requirements for visa applications
to the United Arab Emirates (UAE). During the visa application, the Ministry of Human
Resources and Emiritisation (MOHRE) in the UAE mandates verification of disclosed
information, which often includes sensitive personal information. You mentioned that
verification of the disclosed information to MOHRE is an integral part of the visa application
process to the UAE. However, prior to processing the disclosed personal data, MOHRE does
not secure the consent nor request for a signed Letter of Authorization (LOA) from the data
subjects. The LOA is requested at a later stage, usually after the verification process has
commenced and when the visa processing is already underway.

You now seek clarification on the following:

1) Whether the mandatory requirement to obtain a visa can be considered a lawful basis for
   processing the disclosed information, even in the absence of consent or signed LOA from
   the data subject;

2) Whether the verification process may be based on compliance with a legal obligation, or

Personal information controller versus
Personal information processor;
Obligations of PIC and PIP

Based on the facts provided in the verification process, Dataflow is considered a personal
information processor (PIP)2 since it processes personal and sensitive personal information
(collectively, personal data) of individuals solely upon the documented instructions of the
MOHRE, which is the personal information controller (PIC)3. Accordingly, it is the PIC who
is primarily responsible in establishing the lawful basis for processing. However, the PIP must
ensure that the processing strictly aligns with the documented instructions and lawful basis
provided by the PIC. The PIP shall not process personal data beyond the scope authorized by
the PIC, otherwise the PIP shall be considered a PIC under the DPA.

Verification of information; personal data;
Lawful criteria for processing

Notwithstanding the foregoing, we shall address your inquiry as to the proper basis for
processing sensitive personal information in the context of international migration
requirements. Sections 12 and 13 of the DPA list down the criteria for the lawful processing
of personal information and sensitive personal information, respectively. While verification
of data submitted by an applicant as part of visa requirements may fall under Sections 12 (c),4
(e),5 or (f),6 said legal bases only apply to personal information and not to sensitive personal
information.

Thus, given that the information to be verified also contains sensitive personal information,
at least one criterion under Section 13 of the DPA must be satisfied for the
verification/processing to be considered lawful.

However, as discussed above, it appears that Dataflow is acting as a PIP for MOHRE. In this
capacity, Dataflow may lawfully process the information provided by MOHRE on the basis
of the latter’s documented instructions. It is essential, however, for Dataflow to ensure that
MOHRE’s lawful basis is in accordance with Section 13 of the DPA. Processing sensitive
personal information in the context of visa application may be justified under Section 13 (a)
on consent;7 (b) if provided for by existing laws and regulations; or (f) when provided to
government or public authority. If processing is based on consent under Sec. 13(a), we
recommend checking NPC Circular No. 2023-04 (Guidelines on Consent) to know more on
the requirements on securing a valid and informed consent. If, however, MOHRE’s processing
of sensitive personal information is based on Section 13 (b) or (f), then consent of the data
subject is not required. As such, the belated acquisition of consent or signed LOA would not
be an issue, since consent is not the basis for processing.

General data privacy principles;
Security measures

Under the DPA, PIPs are not exempted from complying with the other provisions of the DPA,
its IRR and related issuances of the NPC. Thus, in addition to the discussions above, the
processing must also strictly adhere to the data privacy principles of transparency,
proportionality, and legitimate purpose as well as the implementation of appropriate and
reasonable security measures.

Specific to the principle of transparency, Dataflow should ensure that the data subjects
involved are informed as to the nature, purpose, and extent of the processing of their personal
data, including the risks and safeguards involved, the identity of the PIC, their rights as data
subjects and how these can be exercised. This may be achieved through the posting of a
privacy notice.8

Moreover, in compliance with the proportionality principle, Dataflow must ensure that the
processing of personal data shall be adequate, relevant, suitable, necessary, and not excessive
in relation to a declared and specified purpose. Personal data shall be processed only if the
purpose of the processing could not reasonably be fulfilled by other means. It necessitates that
only the information requested and necessary for the purpose indicated should be processed.

Most importantly, it is mandatory for all PICs and its PIPs to implement reasonable and
appropriate organizational, physical, and technical security measures for the protection of
personal data.

NPC Circular No. 2023-06 (Security of Personal Data in the Government and the Private
Sector) outlines the minimum standards and requirements for the security of personal data.
A PIC and its PIP must conduct, among other requirements, a Privacy Impact Assessment for
every processing system that involves personal data of individuals. As mentioned in the said
Circular, a PIC and its PIP may implement more detailed or stricter policies and procedures
